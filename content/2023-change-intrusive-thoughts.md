> How do you know I wrote this?

## 2023

In class 12th (2022–23) there was this English lesson in our textbook called *The Last Lesson* by Alphonse Daudet. The story revolves around this kid Franz who is from this little part of France called Alsace. Franz never bothered to focus on his studies and learn his mother tongue French, instead opting to fool around and play games. He shows up to class late one day worrying all the while about whether the teacher would scold him for his tardiness, but when he does end up showing up to the classroom he sees a sight that leaves him perplexed. The class which is usually loud is silent, and along with the students a few of the townsfolk are also in attendance. The teacher, one M. Hamel, somberly asks him to enter the class and defeatedly explains that as Prussia is taking over Alsace, French will no longer be taught in the schools and that this is their last lesson together. Franz is distraught. He regrets wasting his time playing. He wishes that he could turn back time and focus on his French class, knowing now he would never again have the opportunity to study his own mother tongue.

My school days were nearing an end towards the start of 2023, and all the thoughts that occupied my mind concerned my final exams and college. Class went on as usual. I sat in class daydreaming about one thing or another, as I’m sure my classmates did as well. We’d talk about the usual things at lunch—games, girls, and gossip (the original trilogy). Actually, I lied for the sake of alliteration—anime and TV shows were what we were most concerned about.

Our class was the bio batch of our school; this means most of the students left after lunch to go to their coaching centers to “prepare” for the medical entrance exam. The few of us who weren’t in any coaching center were left behind with classes like art, PE, and life skills—classes the school deemed to be pointless. Life was mundane but comfortable, repetitive but joyful, boring but predictable.

## Change

On one such normal school day, as we all have lunch and discuss the usual things, share notes, and plan project reports—amongst discussing the plot of *Breaking Bad*, characters in *Genshin Impact*, and the upcoming school graduation—one of my friends mentions off-handedly that report work will be easy because of this new app called ChatGPT. I didn’t think much of it; I just chalked it up as another fad that is going to come and go like everything else. I personally didn’t bother even checking it out and did my project the usual way—Googling whatever topics concerned the project and copy-pasting the necessary information. It ain’t much but it’s honest work. I never actually used ChatGPT until much later in the year for some college work. I found the app amusing and made an account and whatnot, but in my mind it was nothing more than Google with attitude.

As the days and months passed, ChatGPT and AI in general became more and more ubiquitous, and everything from my WhatsApp chats to my Amazon orders started integrating these bots who were ready to talk to me at any given moment. Assignments in college became easy—just copy the question onto said AI and it would spout out some bullshit you wouldn’t bother reading, and you would simply write this down on an A4 sheet while listening to Red Hot Chili Peppers. Easiest 10 marks I’ve ever scored. This cycle rinsed and repeated. Life went on, and every new day I woke up doing the same old thing as tech giants around the world continued adding these AIs to whatever it is they could add it to.

## My intrusive thoughts

As life went on and exams and assignments went by, and I generated my way through them, life slowly but surely lost meaning. (This is no new thing, though—life is very good at doing this and will do so whenever opportunity strikes.) The exams you had to study for became more fun than the assignments you could generate. The ChatGPT I used every now and then started becoming a sort of Voldemort in my mind—I would prefer it not be named, and I would curse it whenever it was brought up in conversation. My silent partner in all my daily activities—my existentialism—saw this as the perfect topic to think about whenever I was doing anything whatsoever. Whenever and wherever, it was free game to think about AI.

- Watching a movie—how do you know that actor is real?
- Listening to music—how do you know this beat isn’t generated?
- Looking at a piece of art—how do you know someone made this?

And so on and so forth.

Things I would never second guess were now the first thoughts that would pop up in my mind. The worst part about it was that even though the thoughts were internal, their cause was external—something I couldn’t control, yet something that affected me. I went down various spirals trying to cope with this in any way I could. I deleted my accounts in all concerned apps, I de-googled as much as possible, and told others what I thought of the matter. One of my favorite interactions regarding the topic was when my friend, looking over from his laptop after fiddling around with his Cursor IDE at 11 a.m. in the morning in our college hostel, told me with the most deadpan look on his face:

> “You know why you should use AI? Because the people who do will get ahead of you.”

Some days you wake up and you feel like you’re living in a utopia, and most days it feels like a dystopia. This was definitely one of the latter days.

But thinking even further beyond, I realized robots aren’t the only way our lives can be miserable in a Kafkaesque way, and dystopias exist in various different forms. Even though I took my anger out on the software—blaming the AI for ruining my state of mind—the truth is the ones who built and propagate said software are the very humans I wish were singing the songs I was listening to. Maybe humans really love putting each other in these complicated predicaments, and the reason I feel the system or whatever is flawed in the first place is because I am the problem myself, and so is everyone else around me.

My friend told me recently that the only reason these microprocessors that are needed to run these LLMs exist is because the demand for them existed all those years ago. To put it simply, because some kid wanted to play *Doom* in higher graphics back in the ’90s, in some roundabout way, is why I’m dealing with Gemini today. I have no idea how much truth is in that statement, but I agree: one thing leads to another whether we want it to or not.

Even so I can’t help but feel like Franz now, wishing I could extend that French class I thought would always be the same and never go away. Wishing life would always be mundane but comfortable, repetitive but joyful, boring but predictable, and stay that same way.

If tomorrow I walk into a restaurant and Wall-E takes my order and Baymax cooks my food while C-3PO sings songs for ambiance, I’ll eat my food in silence and go home, and to cope with all the madness I’ll put on a Tarantino movie I’ve yet to watch because I know for sure that came out before 2023.

**THIS IS NOT AN EXIT**

But right now… it’s all I got.
